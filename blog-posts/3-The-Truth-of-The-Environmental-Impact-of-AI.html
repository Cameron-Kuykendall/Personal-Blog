Is AI Bad for the Enviroment?

The Hot Topic vs Actual Influential Topic

Lately, AI has become the hot topic in countless debates — from AI-generated art to AI relationships. I have my own concerns about many of these topics. But one issue keeps surfacing: the environmental impact of AI and, more broadly, how modern human life affects the planet.

The Conversation

The concept of normalization tells us this: over time, repeated behaviors, ideas, or systems, no matter how harmful, become accepted as “normal” through repetition and cultural reinforcement. Once something is normalized, we stop questioning it.

So what does this have to do with AI? Everything. Humans tend to look for scapegoats, it’s easier to blame the new and unfamiliar. Headlines like “AI IS DESTROYING THE WORLD” trigger fear and anger, especially when paired with concerns about job loss. It makes sense. But when we react emotionally, we tend to overlook bigger, older problems.

This article is about shining a light on what we often ignore. Let's talk about water, specifically, how we use it.

The Water Problem: The Beef Industry

One of the first criticisms people throw at AI is its water usage, especially for cooling servers. That’s valid, especially in the context of growing water scarcity. But let’s compare that to some of our normalized industries.

Note: These figures are based on global averages. Actual usage varies by region and production methods. Links to articles and information are listed at the bottom

Producing just 1 pound of beef takes anywhere from 441 to 12,000 gallons of water, depending on the method used (Source: Kai Olson-Sawyer, “Beef: The King of the Big Water Footprint”). We’ll use a mid range, and acceptable estimate of 1,800 gallons per pound to keep things conservative. Since 1 lb ≈ 0.45 kg, that comes out to:

13,626 liters per kilogram of beef

Global meat consumption is around 60 million metric tons per year, according to the USDA. That’s about 164,000 metric tons per day.

Total daily water use for meat = 164,000 tons × 1,000 kg/ton × 13,626 liters/kg × 0.264172 gallons/liter
≈ 590,335,658,208 gallons per day

Let that sink in: nearly 600 billion gallons of water used every day for meat — and most people don’t blink an eye. Why? Because it’s normalized. But that leads to a bigger question: how do we even have that much water? The truth is — we kind of don’t.

So where does this water come from?

  • Rainwater: used to grow feed crops
  • Irrigation: pumped from rivers, lakes, aquifers
  • Blue Water: directly drawn from freshwater sources
  • Green Water: rain stored in soil
  • Grey Water: water that’s been used and requires treatment

The problem: A lot of this water is being depleted faster than it can be replenished. Meat agriculture is the #1 driver of aquifer depletion, river drying, and wetland loss.

AI Water Use

Finding hard numbers on AI water use is tough because the industry is still emerging. But one estimate from Shaolei Ren et al., in “Making AI Less Thirsty,” suggests that by 2027, AI could be responsible for 4.2 to 6.6 billion cubic meters of water withdrawals per year.

Let’s use the high end for context:

  • 6.6 billion m³ = 6.6 trillion liters
  • 6.6 trillion liters ≈ 1.7435 trillion gallons/year
  • 1.7435 trillion ÷ 365 ≈ 4.77 billion gallons/day

That sounds like a lot, and no doubt it is. But compared to beef?

Beef: 590 billion gallons/day
AI: 4.77 billion gallons/day

That’s about 120 times more water used for beef. And that doesn’t even include the fact that beef is responsible for roughly 6% of total global greenhouse gas emissions.

Yet the outrage seems focused on AI. Why? Because it's new, not normalized yet, and therefore easier to blame.

So What Now?

If you’re concerned about the environment and think AI is part of the problem, your not wrong. But maybe ask yourself this: Have you cut out beef yet?

I'm not here to tell you what to eat. But if you're going to use environmental impact as an argument, it makes sense to start with the industries doing the most damage.

If you're worried about AI replacing jobs, or the psychological impact of constant automation, that's a different and important conversation. One I might dive into in a future post.
